0.Why transformers came about — motivation

Before we touch a single equation, we need to state what problem the transformer actually solves. Without that, the later formulas are just an incomprehensible tangle of linear algebra. We assume the reader understands classical neural networks, including convolutional ones (CNNs); it is precisely against them that the transformer can be nicely explained as an answer to what earlier architectures did not do well.

A convolutional network has a local receptive field: each neuron sees only a small neighborhood of the input, and global context arises only gradually, by stacking many layers. For images this is ideal, because things that are related tend to be close together. For language, however, it grates — a word at the start of a sentence can determine the meaning of a word at the end, and building such long-range links across dozens of convolutional layers is inefficient.

Before transformers, sequences were processed by recurrent networks (RNNs, LSTMs [ref_4]), which read token by token and carry a "memory". These have two ailments: they are inherently sequential (hard to parallelize on a GPU) and, for long dependencies, the information gets diluted along the way.

The transformer [ref_1] solves both problems with a single idea: let every token look directly at every other token in a single layer, and let the network learn the weights with which it looks at them dynamically, according to content. This is called the attention mechanism. The receptive field thereby becomes global at once and the whole computation parallelizes elegantly (Fig. 1).

1.Tokenization

The problem. The input is plain text — a string of characters. But a neural network computes with numbers, not letters. So the first task is: how do we cut the text into elementary units to which the network will assign numbers? Two extremes suggest themselves, and both have a flaw.

If we took a whole word as the unit, we would get short sequences and a lot of meaning per unit — but the vocabulary of all words is practically infinite. New words, typos, foreign languages, numbers… we will always hit a word that is not in the vocabulary, and the model cannot handle it. If we went to the other extreme and took individual letters, the vocabulary would be tiny and finite, but the sequences would be enormously long and a single letter carries almost no meaning — the model learns from it worse, and the quadratic cost of attention (see below) balloons.

The solution — subword tokens. A golden middle path has become established: tokens are not words, but subword pieces. Frequent strings (common words, endings like "-ing", " the") are glued into a single token; rare words break up into smaller pieces; and absolutely anything can be assembled — in the worst case down to individual bytes. The vocabulary $\mathcal{V}$ is thus finite (typically $|\mathcal{V}|\approx 50\,000$ tokens), yet it covers any text.

$\mathcal{V}$ = the vocabulary (the set of all tokens the model understands); $|\mathcal{V}|$ = its size, the number of tokens.

1.1The BPE algorithm — how the vocabulary is built

Most often the vocabulary is built with the Byte-Pair Encoding (BPE) algorithm [ref_6]. It is a one-off preparation before training the model itself, performed on a large corpus, and the goal is: to discover from the data which character sequences are worth having as standalone tokens. The algorithm is a surprisingly simple loop of "count — merge — repeat":

  1. Start. Cut the whole corpus into atoms (bytes/characters). For now the vocabulary contains only these atoms.
  2. Count. Go through the corpus and count how often each adjacent pair of symbols occurs.
  3. Merge. Take the most frequent pair, glue it into one new token, rewrite all of its occurrences, and add it to the vocabulary (it is stored as a "merge rule").
  4. Repeat steps 2–3 (newly created tokens now count toward new pairs as well) until the vocabulary reaches its target size.

On a tiny corpus with words at frequencies "low" ×5, "newer" ×6, "wider" ×3, "lowest" ×2, the first merge is the pair "e"+"r" (it occurs 9×, the most), so the token "er" is created and "newer" becomes "n e w er". The next round merges "lo" (7×), then "ow", and so on. After thousands of repetitions, longer tokens are glued together out of frequent pieces — common words like " the" or " transform" end up being a single token, whereas rare words remain a few fragments (Fig. 2).

Stopping criterion. In the vast majority of cases the target vocabulary size is simply a hyperparameter chosen in advance; BPE keeps merging until it reaches it. The number of merges is therefore

$$\text{number of merges} \;=\; |\mathcal{V}| \;-\; \underbrace{256}_{\text{byte atoms}} \;-\; (\text{a few special tokens}).$$

The reason for a fixed number is practical: the model needs a fixed-size embedding table and output layer, so we must know the vocabulary size up front. That number is moreover not arbitrary, it is a trade-off: a small vocabulary gives long sequences (a word breaks into more pieces) but a small, well-trained table; a large vocabulary gives short sequences (one word = one token) but a huge table in which rare tokens are undertrained.

1.2Coverage guarantee: byte atoms

One fundamental question remains: what guarantees that the vocabulary covers absolutely every input, when there are theoretically infinitely many words (typos, new words, foreign symbols)? The key is that BPE never throws away the "character extreme" — it just keeps it hidden as a safety net. The algorithm starts from the atomic alphabet and these atomic tokens never disappear from the vocabulary; merging only adds new tokens on top of them. The final vocabulary is therefore always

$$\mathcal{V} \;=\; \{\text{all atoms}\}\;\cup\;\{\text{learned merges}\}.$$

Because the atoms are always available, every conceivable string can be assembled — in the worst case it breaks down to individual atoms. The guarantee is therefore not "we have every word in the list" (that is impossible) but "we have an alphabet from which every word can be composed" — exactly as 26 letters compose infinitely many words.

Modern tokenizers (from GPT-2 onward) work directly with bytes, of which there are exactly 256, and any text in any language (including emoji) is encoded via UTF-8 into a sequence of bytes. With all 256 bytes in the vocabulary, we can represent literally any input. A finite base (256), infinite coverage (Fig. 3).

2.Embeddings: the table E and the matrix X

The problem. We have tokens and their integers (ID = index in the vocabulary). But the ID number on its own carries no meaning — the fact that "cat" has ID 9123 says nothing about what the word means. We need to represent each token by a vector in a space where geometric closeness corresponds to semantic relatedness.

The solution — a learned lookup table. The embedding is a learned table $E$ of size $|\mathcal{V}|\times d$: one row per vocabulary token, each row a $d$-dimensional vector.

$E$ = the embedding table (the reservoir of vectors for all tokens); $d$ = the embedding dimension, i.e. the length of one vector (typically 512 up to thousands).

So "producing an embedding" when using the model means computing nothing: we take the token's ID and pull out the corresponding row of the table. ID 9123 → row 9123 → that is the $d$-dimensional vector. Pure indexing, no function on the input. The values in the table of course have to be computed — but not now and not per token, rather once and for all during training: the vectors in $E$ are learned parameters, random at the start, and the backpropagation learning algorithm gradually tunes them together with the rest of the model, so that tokens used in similar contexts end up with similar vectors (hence the familiar geometries like "king − man + woman ≈ queen") (Fig. 4).

Let us distinguish two different objects, so they do not blur together:

  • The table $E$ — the whole vocabulary, size $|\mathcal{V}|\times d$ (e.g. $50\,000\times512$). A fixed, learned reservoir of all possible tokens; it stays the same in the model. We only read from it.
  • The matrix $X$ — a specific prompt, size $n\times d$ (e.g. $4\times512$). It is formed by pulling out, for each token of the prompt, its single row from $E$ and stacking the rows together. It changes with every prompt.
$X$ = the input matrix of the prompt ($n$ rows = $n$ tokens, each row a $d$-dimensional embedding); $x_i$ = the $i$-th row of $X$, the embedding of the $i$-th token; $n$ = the number of tokens in the prompt.

$X$ is thus a selection of a few rows from the large table $E$ — exactly those corresponding to the prompt's tokens, in the right order (and a token appearing twice in the prompt appears twice in $X$). This matrix $X$ is our starting point: each of its rows so far carries only the context-free, "dictionary" meaning of the token, without a single piece of information about what surrounds it. Breathing context into these vectors is the job of the attention mechanism, which we now turn to.

3.Self-attention — the core of the whole architecture

3.1Goal: from context-free embeddings to contextual ones (X → Z)

Before we compute anything, we must make absolutely plain what we actually want to achieve. Only then will it be clear why the mechanism looks the way it does. (This is the most important paragraph of this whole section — if anything, then this.)

The embeddings in $X$ are context-free. The word "racket" has a single vector in $X$, whether we mean a tennis racket or a noisy disturbance — it is a dictionary average over all meanings of the word. And that is useless, because a word's sense is given only by the sentence. Self-attention takes the matrix $X$ and produces a new matrix $Z$ of the same dimensions ($n\times d$), in which each word has a vector corresponding to what it means right here in this sentence.

$Z$ = the final contextualized matrix (what we want), size $n\times d$; $z_i$ = its $i$-th row, the contextual vector of the $i$-th token. Note: the attention layer itself computes only a contextual correction $C$ (we introduce it in 3.3), and $Z$ arises only by adding it to the original input, $Z = X + C$ (this addition is the residual connection, section 6).

In short: $X$ = words torn out of context, $Z$ = the same words placed into context. Note — attention does not make a "summary of the sentence" into a single vector; it rewrites each individual word according to its surroundings. A row of $X$ is a token out of context, a row of $Z$ is the same token rewritten so that it carries its role in this particular sentence (Fig. 5).

3.2Relevance, not similarity — why the projections WQ, WK

From the goal follows the first question: in order to be rewritten, a token needs to know which words in the sentence it should borrow from. The first, naive idea is: let us compare tokens by the dot product of their embeddings $x_i\cdot x_j$ — the more similar, the larger the weight. This is the "comparison by similarity" intuition and it is a good springboard. But it measures the wrong thing.

The dot product of bare embeddings measures the semantic similarity of words ("are these two words close in meaning?"). Contextualization, however, needs an entirely different relation — relevance: "is token $j$ useful for clarifying token $i$?". And relevance is not the same as similarity.

Take the sentence "Dog bites man". For the verb "bites" to be placed into context, it must pull in its subject ("dog") and its object ("man") — those are the words that determine who bites whom. But a verb and a noun are not similar; their bare embeddings have a small dot product. Conversely, "dog" would by similarity pull in rather "cat" or "wolf" (similar animals), which is useless for understanding the sentence. And "bites" would by similarity match itself best. Bare similarity simply misses the point (Fig. 6).

The solution. A token's embedding is first projected by two learned matrices $W_Q$ and $W_K$ onto two different vectors. Similarity is then measured not in the original meaning space, but in a space the network has trained so that the dot product means "this is relevant to me":

  • query $q_i = x_i W_Q$,
  • key $k_i = x_i W_K$.
$W_Q,W_K$ = learned projection matrices of size $d\times d_k$ (they turn the embedding into a query, resp. a key); $q_i,k_i$ = the query and key of the $i$-th token; $d_k$ = the query/key dimension.

A second, even stronger reason for the projections: the bare product $XX^{\top}$ has no learnable parameter. The network could not learn how to weight attention — the pattern would be fixed by the embeddings and identical in every layer and head. The matrices $W_Q,W_K$ are the only place where the scoring is learned at all. And because $W_Q\ne W_K$, the resulting relation is moreover directional: a pronoun may look at its noun more than the noun looks at it — which the symmetric $XX^{\top}$ cannot do by any means (we will return to this in 3.5).

3.3Mechanics: query, key, value, score, softmax, weighted sum

Now that we know why the projections exist, we can write out the whole computation without worry. Besides the query and key we introduce a third projection — the value $v_i = x_i W_V$ — which determines what a token actually passes on (why separately, we explain in 3.6). In matrix form, for the whole sequence at once:

$$Q = X W_Q,\qquad K = X W_K,\qquad V = X W_V.$$

$Q,K,V$ = the matrices of queries, keys and values (row = one token); $W_V$ = the learned value projection, of size $d\times d_v$; $V$ here denotes the value matrix (not to be confused with the vocabulary size, which we consistently write $|\mathcal{V}|$).

Score. For each pair of tokens we compute how relevant token $j$ is for token $i$, as the scaled dot product of the query with the key:

$$S = \frac{QK^{\top}}{\sqrt{d_k}}\in\mathbb{R}^{n\times n},\qquad S_{ij} = \frac{q_i\cdot k_j}{\sqrt{d_k}}.$$

$S$ = the score matrix ($n\times n$); $S_{ij}$ = the relevance of token $j$ for token $i$; the meaning of $\sqrt{d_k}$ is discussed in 3.4.

Softmax. We apply the softmax function to each row of $S$, obtaining a weight matrix $A$ with non-negative entries whose row sums are 1:

$$A = \mathrm{softmax}(S),\qquad a_{ij} = \frac{e^{S_{ij}}}{\sum_{j'} e^{S_{ij'}}}.$$

$A$ = the attention-weight matrix; $a_{ij}$ = the weight with which token $i$ takes from token $j$. The softmax turns relevance into a competition: each token distributes 100 % of its attention among all the others.

Weighted sum. The raw output of the attention layer for token $i$ is the weighted average of the values of all tokens, where the weights are exactly $A$. We will call this output the contextual correction $c_i$ (the matrix of them is denoted $C$):

$$c_i = \sum_j a_{ij}\,v_j,\qquad\text{i.e. in matrix form}\qquad C = A\,V.$$

$C$ = the raw output of the attention layer (the contextual correction), size $n\times d$; $c_i$ = its $i$-th row. Mind the distinction we keep throughout the text: $C$ is only the correction the attention layer computes, whereas the final contextualized representation $Z$ arises only by adding it to the original input, $Z = X + C$ (this addition is the residual connection, in detail in section 6). Within section 3 we are mostly interested in the correction $C$ itself.

All of it can be written in a single line — the function $\mathrm{Attn}(X)$ returning the correction $C$, the full definition of (single-head) self-attention:

$$C = \mathrm{Attn}(X) = \mathrm{softmax}\!\left(\frac{X W_Q (X W_K)^{\top}}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right)X W_V,\qquad Z = X + C.$$

So next to "player", "bat" stretches toward the sports meaning, next to "cave" toward the animal one. The meaning disambiguation from Fig. 5 is not a side effect — it is literally what this weighted sum computes (Fig. 7).

3.4Scaling by √dk

It remains to explain that divisor $\sqrt{d_k}$. It is not cosmetics but a necessity. In high dimension $d_k$ the dot product of two random vectors typically grows proportionally to $\sqrt{d_k}$, so without scaling the scores would take on large values. On large inputs the softmax "saturates" — almost all weight jumps onto a single token and practically zero remains for the others. Where the softmax is saturated, its gradient is close to zero, so the network would stop learning. Dividing by $\sqrt{d_k}$ brings the values back into a reasonable range and keeps the gradient healthy.

Heuristic: if the components of $q_i,k_j$ have unit variance, then $q_i\cdot k_j$ has variance of order $d_k$; dividing by $\sqrt{d_k}$ brings the variance back to order 1.

3.5Directionality and self-scores — the bilinear form M

This subsection is a deeper supplementary explanation and can be skipped on a first reading; it does, however, clarify two things that often confuse students: why the comparison is directional (asymmetric) and why a token does not automatically start attending mainly to itself.

Let us expand the score (without scaling) using the definitions $Q=XW_Q$, $K=XW_K$:

$$QK^{\top} = X W_Q (X W_K)^{\top} = X \underbrace{\big(W_Q W_K^{\top}\big)}_{M}\, X^{\top}.$$

The whole score matrix is thus a bilinear form $XMX^{\top}$ with a single learned matrix $M = W_Q W_K^{\top}\in\mathbb{R}^{d\times d}$.

$M$ = the bilinear score matrix. The matrices $W_Q,W_K$ separately are just a low-rank factorization of $M$ into dimension $d_k$ — no deeper magic.

Directionality. Because $M$ is in general not symmetric, we have $S_{ij}\ne S_{ji}$ — "how much sat looks at cat" can be an entirely different number than "how much cat looks at sat". With cosine similarity (and with bare $XX^{\top}$) both numbers would have to be equal. Incidentally, this is why attention is not pure cosine similarity: the cosine normalizes the lengths away and cares only about the angle, whereas the dot product $q\cdot k=\lVert q\rVert\,\lVert k\rVert\cos\theta$ carries the lengths too — a token can have a "loud" key with a large norm and attract attention even without perfect alignment of direction.

$\theta$ = the angle between vectors; $\lVert\cdot\rVert$ = the norm (length) of a vector.

Self-scores. Students often suspect that after the projection the "correlation of a word with itself" drops to zero. The diagnosis is correct, the conclusion overshot. Without the projection the score would be $S=XX^{\top}$, so on the diagonal sits $S_{ii}=\lVert x_i\rVert^2$, which by the Cauchy–Schwarz inequality is the row maximum — every token would look mainly at itself (a degeneracy). The projection does indeed remove this degeneracy, but it does not replace it with an enforced zero. The self-score is now $S_{ii}=x_i M x_i^{\top}$, the value of a quadratic form, which can come out large positive, negative, or small. Let us decompose $M$ into a symmetric and an antisymmetric part:

$$M = M_{\text{sym}} + M_{\text{anti}},\qquad M_{\text{sym}}=\tfrac12(M+M^{\top}),\quad M_{\text{anti}}=\tfrac12(M-M^{\top}).$$

For the antisymmetric part we have $x M_{\text{anti}} x^{\top}=0$ for every $x$, so to the diagonal (and thereby to all self-scores) only the symmetric part contributes, whereas all of the directionality is carried solely by the antisymmetric one:

$$S_{ii} = x_i\,M_{\text{sym}}\,x_i^{\top},\qquad S_{ij}-S_{ji} = 2\,x_i\,M_{\text{anti}}\,x_j^{\top}.$$

The self-score would therefore be zero only in the degenerate case $M_{\text{sym}}\approx0$, which training has no reason to produce. In practice strong self-attention is common and useful (a token carries its own value onward). Some heads have a dominant diagonal, others a suppressed one — it is learned, not forced either to the maximum or to zero (Fig. 8).

3.6Why the projection WV as well

One last question of mechanics remains: we have the weights $A$ — why don't we apply them straight to the bare $X$ (i.e. $AX$), but only to $XW_V$? There are two compelling reasons.

1. Expressivity. If we computed $AX$, every output row would be a reweighted average of the original embeddings. Because the weights are non-negative and sum to 1, the output is always a convex combination of the input rows — it never leaves the space of the input vectors. The layer would only be able to reshuffle what it already has, and by stacking layers we would keep averaging the same vectors over and over. The projection $W_V$ gives the content path its own learned weights, so the output lives in a learned space, not merely in the convex hull of the inputs.

2. Separation of two roles. The matrix $A$ governs whom you listen to (that came out of $Q,K$). What you take from them is an independent question — and $W_V$ is precisely the layer for "what gets passed on". Without it, "by what I pair up" and "what actually flows" would be the same vector, even though they are two roles we want to keep separate (Fig. 9).

3.7A neural-network view: dense layers + dynamic routing

For the reader whom matrix terminology confuses more than it helps, there is a better mental model — and for a neural-network expert it is even more natural. Multiplying $XW$ with a matrix $W$ of size, say, $512\times64$ is in fact nothing other than a dense (linear) layer with 64 neurons, no bias and no activation, which we run each token (row) through separately. The entries of the matrix are the synaptic weights of that layer. There is no other magic in it. This parallel — multiplication by a matrix ⟷ passing through a linear layer of neurons — we will use throughout the rest of the text alongside the dry formulas.

Self-attention thus translates into four small dense layers and two non-traditional operations between them:

  • three layers ($W_Q,W_K,W_V$) run the token's embedding through and produce three activations from it — query, key, value (shared across positions, exactly like a $1\times1$ convolution);
  • the fourth layer ($W_O$, see section 4) comes at the end.

And now the essential thing — two operations in attention are not dense layers, and they are precisely the only new thing the transformer brings over an ordinary feedforward network:

  1. The score $q\cdot k$ is the dot product of the activations of two different tokens — no learnable weights, just a comparison.
  2. The weighted sum of values combines the values with weights that are the output of the softmax, i.e. computed at run time, not frozen after training.

In an ordinary dense layer the weights are fixed after training; here the mixing weights are recomputed afresh for each input. To this dynamic weight-free routing the transformer owes all of its power (Fig. 10).

3.8A step-by-step example: "Mom grinds meat"

Why one more example. We built the whole mechanism in general. Now, though, we will run it on a single, deliberately trivial sentence "Mom grinds meat" — three words, three tokens — and go through absolutely every intermediate step with concrete numbers. The goal is for the reader to see how context arises from bare embeddings, and to be able to recompute everything with a pencil. Each word has its own colour: Mom, grinds, meat.

Three roles of one word (a reminder). From the embedding of each word we produce its three projections from section 3.3: the query $q$ ("what am I looking for?"), the key $k$ ("by what will others find me?") and the value $v$ ("what do I pass on?"). In the attention matrix the row is the word that asks (its $q$), and the column is the word it looks at (its $k$). The whole pipeline on our sentence looks like this (Fig. 11).

Nine views: who looks at whom. We compare each query with each key via a dot product — out comes a square 3×3 table, the attention matrix $A$. In the interactive Fig. 12 below, click (or hover) on a word and you will see how it distributes its attention; the numbers in the cells are already the finished weights after softmax, so each row sums to 1. In small print below them is also the raw score before softmax.

From raw score to weights. The scores in the cells are just numbers; in two steps we turn them into probability weights. First scaling by dividing by $\sqrt{d_k}$ (here $d_k=2$), then softmax, which turns a row into positive numbers summing to 1. Written out for the row grinds:

score  =  [ 0.70, 1.16, 1.28 ]
÷ √2  =  [ 0.50, 0.82, 0.91 ]
exp   =  [ 1.64, 2.27, 2.47 ]  (sum 6.38)
softmax =  [ 0.26, 0.36, 0.39 ] Each number is the ratio of exp(score) to the sum of the whole row — that is why the weights are never negative and always sum to one. This is exactly the row grinds in the matrix above.

The weighted sum: the contextual correction. We finally use the weights from the row. The raw output of the attention layer for a word is the weighted average of the values $V$ of all words — as much attention, so much influence. We call this output the correction and denote it $c$. For grinds:

c(grinds) = 0.26 · v(Mom)  +  0.36 · v(grinds)  +  0.39 · v(meat)

Because grinds gave the most weight to meat, its correction is the most "stretched" toward meat — the word has mixed into itself the information about its object. We do the same weighted sum for each row and obtain the whole correction matrix $C$ (row $i$ is the correction $c_i$ for the $i$-th word).

Adding to the original word: the birth of $Z$. One last little thing, which we develop in section 6: the correction $c$ is added to the original embedding of the word (the residual connection), and only thereby does the definitive contextualized vector $z$ arise. The word thus keeps its identity and in addition gains information about its surroundings (this is the $X\to Z$ from section 3.1):

z(grinds) = c(grinds) + e(grinds)

The whole pass in numbers. So that everything can be checked with a pencil, we substitute small two-dimensional embeddings and, for clarity, set the projections $W_Q=W_K=W_V=I$ (the identity matrix), so that $q=k=v=e$. The core — dot product, softmax, weighted sum — stays the same. 4.Multi-head attention and the projection WO

The problem. One head is exactly the mechanism from section 3: one triple $W_Q,W_K,W_V$, that is, one learned relation $M=W_QW_K^{\top}$ — one definition of "what is relevant to whom". The catch is that the output for a token is one weighted sum, and because of the softmax the weights sum to one, so different relations fight over that budget. When "bites" pours 0.9 of the weight onto its subject "dog", only 0.1 is left for the object "man". One head can express only one mixture.

But a word relates to the others in several ways at once: a verb needs the subject and the object, plus perhaps tense and adverb; a pronoun needs its antecedent. Cramming this into a single softmax means averaging it and losing part of the information.

The solution — several heads in parallel. Instead of one triple of matrices, $h$ triples are used, each projecting into a smaller dimension $d_k=d/h$, and each head has its own softmax, hence its own budget and its own relation. One head learns "look at the subject", another "look at the object", and they do not get in each other's way.

$h$ = the number of heads (typically 8, 16…); each head has dimension $d_k=d/h$ (e.g. $512/8=64$).

Mathematically it is just the computation from 3.3 repeated $h$ times and sewn together at the end:

$$\mathrm{head}_i=\mathrm{softmax}\!\left(\frac{(XW_Q^i)(XW_K^i)^{\top}}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right)X W_V^i,\qquad \mathrm{MultiHead}(X)=\mathrm{Concat}(\mathrm{head}_1,\dots,\mathrm{head}_h)\,W_O,$$

where each $W_\bullet^i\in\mathbb{R}^{d\times d_k}$ and $W_O\in\mathbb{R}^{d\times d}$.

$\mathrm{head}_i$ = the output of the $i$-th head ($n\times d_k$); the superscript $i$ on $W_Q^i$ etc. means "the matrices belonging to the $i$-th head"; $W_O$ = the output projection; $\mathrm{Concat}$ = concatenation (gluing) of the head outputs column-wise.

Two things are worth emphasizing. First, the input dimension of a token stays $d$ — it is not divided by eight. What gets divided by eight is only the output width of the projection layers. Mind the nuance: the input of each head is still the full $d$; the head does not get a "slice" of the token (the first 64 numbers), but the whole $d$-dimensional token, and through its own layer compresses it into its $d_k$. Eight heads thus read the same token, only each through different weights into a different subspace. Second, multi-head is not $h$ times more expensive than one big head: the dimension $d$ is just cut into $h$ subspaces, so eight heads of 64 have together $512\times512$ weights for each of the Q/K/V layers — exactly as many as a single $512\to512$ layer. We are not adding capacity, just dividing it into independent relations (Fig. 13).

What $W_O$ is for. Notice that after concatenation the dimension already fits ($h\cdot d_k = d$) — so $W_O$ is not there for the sake of shape. The problem is elsewhere: in bare concatenation the head blocks are merely laid side by side and unmixed. Columns 1–64 belong exclusively to head 1, columns 65–128 to head 2, etc. — they have never met one another. The matrix $W_O$ ($d\times d$) is that learned mixing. It is seen most cleanly from the expansion: split $W_O$ into row blocks $W_O^i$ (one per head), then

$$\mathrm{Concat}(\mathrm{head}_1,\dots,\mathrm{head}_h)\,W_O \;=\; \sum_{i=1}^{h}\mathrm{head}_i\,W_O^i.$$

Each head, with its block $W_O^i$, "lifts" itself from its $d_k$-dimensional subspace back into the full $d$, and all contributions are summed. $W_O$ thus does two things at once: it writes each head back into a shared space and lets them combine — the network can amplify a useful head and suppress a redundant one. This nicely complements the "read/write" pair: $W_V^i$ determines what the head reads from the sequence, $W_O^i$ determines how what is read is written back.

5.Positional encoding

A hidden flaw that needs to be exposed. Let us return to the core of attention and ask an uncomfortable question that we have so far elegantly forgotten: does self-attention even know in what order the tokens stand? The score between two positions $i$ and $j$ arose as $q_i\cdot k_j$ — the dot product of one token's query and another's key. In this computation, however, neither the difference $i-j$ nor the absolute value of the indices appears anywhere. The token "Bob" sees the token "Alice" in exactly the same way, whether Alice stands right before him or ten words away. Attention treats the input as a set, not as a sequence.

Formally: permutation equivariance. Let $P$ denote an arbitrary permutation matrix  $P$ — a square $n\times n$ matrix that has a single one in each row and column and zeros otherwise; multiplication $PX$ merely rearranges the rows of the matrix $X$, i.e. shuffles the tokens. Then for the whole attention layer we have

$$\mathrm{Attn}(PX) \;=\; P\,\mathrm{Attn}(X).$$

In words: if I shuffle the input tokens, I get the same outputs, only in the same shuffled order. So attention is not entirely blind to order — the order of the outputs copies the order of the inputs — but the content of each output vector does not depend on the order. And that is a catastrophe: the sentences "Alice calls Bob" and "Bob calls Alice" contain an identical set of three tokens, so attention would produce from them (up to rearrangement) the same context vectors. Who calls whom would be lost (Fig. 14).

What exactly we want to solve. We need to add to each token a "stamp" of its position such that:

(a) two identical words at different places receive a different stamp (otherwise we cannot tell "Alice calls Bob" from "Bob calls Alice");
(b) the stamp is bounded — it must not grow beyond all limits with the sentence length, otherwise for long sequences it would overpower the word's meaning itself;
(c) the model can read from the stamps not only the absolute position, but above all the relative distances ("two words further on"), because grammatical relations are typically relative.

The original transformer's solution: sinusoidal encoding. The authors [ref_1] proposed assigning to each position $i$ ($i=0,1,2,\dots$) a positional vector $p_i\in\mathbb{R}^{d}$ of the same dimension as the embedding, whose components are the values of sines and cosines of various frequencies. The trick is that we pair up the $d$ coordinates of this vector two by two: the $m$-th pair is formed by the coordinates with indices $2m$ and $2m+1$, and we fill it with the sine and cosine of one and the same frequency. So for the pair with index $m$ ($m=0,1,\dots,d/2-1$) we have

$$p_{i,\,2m} \;=\; \sin\!\Big(\frac{i}{10000^{\,2m/d}}\Big), \qquad p_{i,\,2m+1} \;=\; \cos\!\Big(\frac{i}{10000^{\,2m/d}}\Big).$$

$i$ — the token's position in the sequence (which word in order it is). The positional vector $p_i$ has $d$ coordinates, just like the embedding; we split these $d$ coordinates into $d/2$ pairs, and $m$ is the ordinal number of such a pair ($m=0,1,\dots,d/2-1$). Pair $m$ is formed by two specific neighbouring coordinates of the vector: the even one with index $2m$ (which gets the sine) and the odd one with index $2m+1$ (which gets the cosine); both share the same frequency $1/10000^{\,2m/d}$. So $m$ is the "frequency-channel number", not directly a single coordinate — one $m$ always stands for two coordinates (its sine and cosine ones). $d$ — the embedding dimension; the constant $10000$ sets the range of wavelengths from short to very long.

We then simply add this vector to the token's embedding, so that $x_i + p_i$ enters the attention layer instead of the bare $x_i$. This breaks the permutation symmetry: the same word at position 2 and at position 7 receives a different shift, and hence a different query and key.

Why sinusoids in particular — and why there are several. Condition (b) they satisfy automatically: $\sin$ and $\cos$ always stay in the interval $[-1,1]$, however large the position. Condition (c) is ensured by the fact that there is a whole range of frequencies. The lowest $m$ gives a rapidly changing wave (neighbouring positions differ markedly — fine resolution of nearby words), the highest $m$ gives a very slow wave (it changes only over hundreds of positions — coarse resolution of distant stretches). It is like writing a number in a positional numeral system: the units change fast, the highest orders slowly, and only all the orders together uniquely determine the value. Thanks to this set of scales the model can cover short and long dependencies at the same time (Fig. 15).

The key property: a shift in position = a rotation. Why precisely the pair (sine, cosine)? Because then each frequency component has the form of a point on a circle, $(\sin\theta_i,\cos\theta_i)$, and a shift by $\Delta$ positions means a rotation of the angle by a fixed increment $\Delta\cdot\omega_m$.  $\omega_m=1/10000^{\,2m/d}$ — the angular velocity of the $m$-th pair; $\theta_i=i\,\omega_m$. The relation between positions $i$ and $i+\Delta$ is thus in each channel the same rotation regardless of where in the sequence we are — it depends only on the distance $\Delta$, not on the absolute location. This is exactly condition (c): the model can learn the operator "look two positions back" as a single fixed linear map, usable anywhere.

A more modern variant: RoPE. Sinusoidal encoding is added to the embedding at the input. The newer rotary positional encoding — RoPE [ref_9] — takes the idea "position = rotation" all the way: instead of adding a vector, it directly rotates the pairs of components of the query $q_i$ and the key $k_j$ by an angle proportional to their position, still inside the score computation. The dot product $q_i\cdot k_j$ then depends on the positions only through their difference $i-j$ — relative distance is thus built directly into the mechanics of attention, not merely offered as an input admixture. Thanks to this, RoPE generalizes better to longer sequences than the model saw during training, and has become the standard in a number of today's large models (e.g. the LLaMA family).

6.The residual connection and its deeper meaning

Motivation: the attention layer should not replace the result but supplement it. The attention layer gives us the correction $c_i$ (a row of the matrix $C$). The question arises how to use it — should we replace the original vector $x_i$ with it? But that is needlessly harsh. The token's embedding already carries a lot that is useful (its bare meaning); the attention layer is only meant to add context to it. If we overwrote the original representation in every layer, we would risk losing along the way through the deep network even what was already correct. It is more natural to let the layer compute only a correction and add it to the input.

The residual connection. So the output of a sublayer is not used on its own, but as an increment to the input:

$$Z \;=\; X \;+\; C,\qquad C=\mathrm{Attn}(X).$$

The layer thus does not model the target representation $\mathcal{H}(X)$ directly, but only its deviation from the input, the residual $\mathcal{F}(X)$.  $\mathcal{H}$ — the desired target map from input to output; $\mathcal{F}$ — what the layer actually learns, so that $\mathcal{H}(X)=X+\mathcal{F}(X)$. We use these two calligraphic letters only here. If for some token the best thing is to "do nothing", it suffices for the layer to learn $\mathcal{F}\approx 0$ — i.e. a zero correction, which is far easier than teaching a dense map to reproduce the input unchanged.

The residual stream. Because each sublayer (attention and the feed-forward network, see below) adds, there arises across the whole depth of the network a kind of backbone "highway" — the residual stream — through which information passes, and the individual layers only gradually write their contributions into it. The gradient can propagate directly along this highway during learning (addition has derivative 1), which is why transformer networks can be trained even to tens and hundreds of layers without the signal dying out along the way.

Historical origin. This trick was not invented by the authors of the transformer. It comes from residual networks (ResNet) [ref_2], which were the first to show that very deep convolutional networks can be trained precisely when, instead of a direct map, we let the layers model the residual and add shortcut ("skip") connections. A related idea — learned gates controlling how much information passes through unchanged — was carried by Highway Networks [ref_3] and even earlier by the forget gate in the LSTM [ref_4] (Fig. 16).

The universal principle "base + correction". It is worth seeing that the same pattern — take a rough estimate and add to it a small correction — is one of the deepest and most repeated motifs in all of mathematics and physics. It is no accident; it is a way of making the complex manageable:

  • Perturbation theory in physics: we compute the energy of a complex system as the energy of a simple solvable system plus a small perturbation.
  • The Taylor expansion: we express the value of a function near a point as the value at the point plus successive higher-order corrections.
  • Newton's method and the Kalman filter: new estimate = old estimate plus a correction term driven by the error.
  • Gradient boosting: a strong model = the sum of many weak ones, where each next one only corrects the error of the previous ones.

The residual block does exactly the same in the space of representations: it carries the current best estimate of the meaning, and each layer refines it a little.

A continuous view: Neural ODE. This analogy can be taken to a beautiful conclusion. If we write one layer as $Z=X+\mathcal{F}(X)$, it is exactly one Euler step of the numerical solution of the differential equation $\tfrac{dh}{d\ell}=\mathcal{F}(h)$ with step size 1.  $h$ — the representation understood as a state that evolves; $\ell$ — the layer index in the role of "time". A deep residual network is then a discretization of a continuous flow in which the representation smoothly "transforms" from input to output. This view is formalized by Neural ODEs [ref_5] and explains why adding further layers acts more like a finer integration of the same process than like gluing together unrelated transformations.

7.The feed-forward network (FFN)

What the attention layer cannot do. Let us return to what the attention layer actually does: each output vector is a weighted average of the values $v_j$ of the other tokens. However cleverly we choose the weights, it is still only a linear combination of the vectors read — a mixing, not real processing. If we stacked nothing but such layers one after another, the whole model would remain (up to the softmax in the weights) essentially a linear mixing and would not be able to realize more complex decisions of the type "if this is a subject and at the same time the verb is in the past tense, then …". For that we need a nonlinearity and a computation that reforges each token on its own.

The solution: a feed-forward network applied to each token separately. So after the attention layer a feed-forward network (FFN) is inserted — a small two-layer neural network applied to each token independently (the same weights for all positions). It has the shape "expand → nonlinearity → contract":

$$\mathrm{FFN}(x) \;=\; \mathrm{GELU}\!\big(x\,W_1+b_1\big)\,W_2+b_2.$$

$W_1$ — the matrix expanding the dimension from $d$ to $d_{\text{ff}}$; $b_1$ — the corresponding bias (shift); $\mathrm{GELU}$ — a smooth nonlinearity (see below); $W_2$ — the matrix contracting the dimension from $d_{\text{ff}}$ back to $d$; $b_2$ — the bias of the second layer. The hidden width $d_{\text{ff}}$ is usually $\approx 4d$ (e.g. $d=512\Rightarrow d_{\text{ff}}=2048$).

Why expand and then contract again. The first layer projects the $d$-dimensional vector into a much wider space of $d_{\text{ff}}\approx 4d$ dimensions. Each of those $d_{\text{ff}}$ neurons works as a detector of one feature: it computes the dot product of the input with its weight vector, and the nonlinearity then decides whether the feature has "fired" or not. Only in such an expanded space is there enough room for a rich set of specialized detectors to arise (e.g. "this is a unit of mass", "this word is a verb of motion", "the context is technical"). The second layer then assembles a correction from the active detectors and returns it to the $d$-dimensional space (Fig. 17).

The nonlinearity: ReLU and GELU. Without a nonlinearity between the two layers, $W_1$ and $W_2$ would merge into a single linear map and the whole expansion would lose its point. The classic choice is ReLU, $\mathrm{ReLU}(a)=\max(0,a)$ — it passes the positive part and clips the negative one to zero, so a detector is either "on" or "off". Today's models mostly use the smoother GELU, which transitions continuously around zero and in practice trains a little better; to grasp the principle one can picture it as a "soft ReLU".

A concrete example. Imagine that the context from the attention layer has hinted that the token "crane" appears in the engineering sense (a lifting machine), not as a bird. One of the detectors in the first layer may be sensitive precisely to the combination of features "crane + technical context" and it fires. The second layer then, on the basis of this firing, writes into the representation a correction shifting the meaning toward "machine". Thus the FFN writes onto the token features that do not follow from mere mixing of neighbours.

The FFN as a key–value memory. This reasoning can be generalized: the rows of $W_1$ work as keys (each detects a certain pattern in the input) and the corresponding columns of $W_2$ as values (what gets written upon detection). The FFN is thus in fact an associative memory stored in the weights — not for nothing does it usually hold the majority of the parameters of the whole model (thanks to the factor $d_{\text{ff}}\approx 4d$ the pair $W_1,W_2$ has on the order of $8d^2$ parameters, whereas the whole quadruple of attention projections has only $4d^2$). A large part of the model's "knowledge" thus does not reside in the attention layer, but precisely here.

Just as with the attention layer, the FFN's output too enters the residual stream as an added correction, not as a replacement: $Z=X+\mathrm{FFN}(X)$.

8.Normalization: LayerNorm and RMSNorm

Motivation: keep the numbers at a reasonable scale. The residual stream has one unpleasant property — each layer adds to it, so the magnitude of the vectors tends to grow gradually. On top of that the range of values changes with the input. If the numbers entering the attention layer or the FFN fluctuate uncontrollably (one moment around $0{.}1$, another around $50$), training becomes unstable: gradients either explode or vanish, the softmax saturates, and the network learns with difficulty. We therefore need, before each sublayer, to pull the representation down to a stable, predictable scale.

LayerNorm. The standard solution is layer normalization [ref_16]. For each token separately it computes the mean $\mu$ and the standard deviation $\sigma$ over the $d$ components of its vector and rescales the vector to zero mean and unit variance:

$$\hat{x} \;=\; \frac{x-\mu}{\sqrt{\sigma^2+\varepsilon}}, \qquad \mathrm{LN}(x) \;=\; \gamma\odot\hat{x}+\beta.$$

$\mu=\frac1d\sum_k x_k$ — the mean of the components of the given token; $\sigma^2=\frac1d\sum_k (x_k-\mu)^2$ — their variance; $\varepsilon$ — a small constant guarding against division by zero; $\gamma$ — a learned scale vector (component-wise); $\beta$ — a learned shift vector; $\odot$ — component-wise multiplication. Everything is computed per token over its own $d$ components, independently of the other tokens.

Isn't that circular? First we standardize, then we scale and shift again. At first glance it looks pointless: we pull the vector to mean 0 and variance 1 — and immediately the parameters $\gamma,\beta$ rescale and shift it again. The key is that these are two different freedoms. Standardization removes the input-dependent, unpredictable fluctuation of scale (the one that changes from token to token and from batch to batch). The parameters $\gamma$ and $\beta$ then supply a stable, learned and input-independent scale and shift, which are fixed for each component after training. So we are replacing wild, uncontrolled fluctuation with a controlled scale that the network can learn to set optimally — this is not a circle, but an exchange of chaos for order (Fig. 18).

RMSNorm. A newer and today very widespread variant, RMSNorm [ref_17], simplifies the whole procedure: it omits both the subtraction of the mean and the shift $\beta$ and merely divides the vector by its effective magnitude (the square root of the mean of the squares, RMS):

$$\mathrm{RMSNorm}(x) \;=\; \frac{x}{\sqrt{\frac1d\sum_k x_k^2+\varepsilon}}\odot\gamma.$$

It turned out that the main stabilizing effect is provided precisely by the control of magnitude, not by centring at zero. RMSNorm is therefore cheaper (fewer operations, fewer parameters) and in practice works comparably — which is why many current large models choose it.

Pre-norm vs post-norm. It remains to decide where to place the normalization within the block. The original transformer put it after the sublayer (post-norm). Today's models almost always place it before the sublayer (pre-norm): $Z=X+\mathrm{Attn}(\mathrm{LN}(X))$. The advantage is that the residual "highway" then stays completely clean — normalization is applied only to the branch that computes the correction, whereas the main stream passes through unchanged. This makes deep networks markedly more stable to train. At the very end of the stack one more final normalization is usually added before the representation is sent to the output.

9.The whole transformer block

Now we have all the pieces and can assemble them. A transformer block consists of two sublayers, each of which is wrapped in a residual connection and a preceding normalization (pre-norm):

$$Y \;=\; X + \mathrm{MultiHead}\!\big(\mathrm{LN}(X)\big), \qquad Z \;=\; Y + \mathrm{FFN}\!\big(\mathrm{LN}(Y)\big).$$

$X$ — the block's input (an $n\times d$ matrix); $Y$ — the intermediate result after the attention sublayer; $Z$ — the output of the whole block; $n$ — the number of tokens; $d$ — the embedding dimension.

The division of labour is clean and worth repeating:

  • Attention provides communication between tokens — each token reads the relevant information from the others (mixing across positions).
  • The FFN provides computation within a token — it processes each position separately and nonlinearly and writes in features that do not follow from mixing.
  • The residual connection holds a running estimate of the meaning, and both sublayers only write corrections into it.
  • Normalization keeps the numbers at a stable scale so that the whole stack can be trained at all.

The shape of the block's input and output is the same ($n\times d$). This is essential: the blocks can therefore be stacked to any depth, the output of one being the input of the next (Fig. 19).

10.From a block to a language model

10.1A stack of layers and the qualitative leap Z1 → ZN

A single transformer block can do one "quantum step" of processing. A real model stacks $N$ of them one after another (on the order of tens to hundreds). Let us denote the output of the $\ell$-th layer $Z_\ell$, so that

$$X=Z_0 \;\xrightarrow{\text{block }1}\; Z_1 \;\xrightarrow{\text{block }2}\; Z_2 \;\to\;\cdots\;\to\; Z_N.$$

$\ell$ — the layer index ($\ell=1,\dots,N$); $N$ — the total number of blocks; $Z_\ell$ — the $n\times d$ matrix of representations at the output of the $\ell$-th layer.

The qualitative leap. Between $Z_1$ and $Z_N$ lies not just "more of the same". Empirically, lower layers capture rather surface-level and local phenomena (word shape, part of speech, the nearest relations), whereas higher layers represent ever more abstract and contextual content (the role in the sentence, pronoun resolution, the meaning of a whole phrase, intent). A token's representation changes along the way through the stack from "what word it is" to "what role that word plays in precisely this context and what follows from it". This is why a single block does not suffice: depth allows meaning to be built layer by layer, much as a perturbation correction upon correction refines an estimate.

10.2The causal mask

Motivation: why mask at all. We train the model to predict the next token — more precisely, to predict the token that should follow the prompt we have had the neural network (LLM) process. The task "guess the next token" by itself does not enforce a causal mask — bidirectional attention over the prompt would work without any problem (all tokens within the processed prompt see one another). The reason why the causal mask is nevertheless used almost always in these predictive models is twofold: learning efficiency and the possibility of a KV-cache.

Reason 1 — from a single sentence straight to $n$ training examples. A crucial trick in training language models is that from one prompt composed of $n$ tokens one extracts not one but straight away $n$ training examples at once. This is realized by requiring of the neural network (primarily during learning), for each $k$, that on the basis of the representation of the $k$-th token (the $k$-th row of the final matrix $Z_N$) it predict the token that stands at position $k+1$ in the prompt. For this to be fair and to contribute correctly to learning the given skill, the representation of position $k$ must not "see" token $k+1$ (nor any later one) or its representation, and this throughout the whole course of processing the prompt — otherwise the answer would be known in advance and the neural network would learn nothing from these training examples. This is exactly what the causal mask ensures: it guarantees that the representation at position $k$ depends only on tokens $1,\dots,k$ (or their representations). With a single pass of the network we then compute the neural network's error for all $n$ positions at once, instead of running the network separately for each prefix length. Without the mask we would obtain from one prompt only one usable example (the last position); with the mask we have $n$ of them, and learning is thus orders of magnitude more efficient.

Example — one pass instead of several

Take a short prompt of $n=4$ tokens: Cat crawls out <eos>.

Without a causal mask we would have to feed the network successively growing prefixes — i.e. three separate forward passes:

  1. input Cat  →  prediction crawls
  2. input Cat crawls  →  prediction out
  3. input Cat crawls out  →  prediction <eos>

With a causal mask we send the whole sentence at once to the network in a single pass. The mask behaves like a "one-way mirror" in the weight matrix: each position sees only itself and its predecessors, so from its representation one can fairly predict the following token:

  • position 1 (Cat) sees only itself → here the network generates the prediction for position 2;
  • position 2 (crawls) sees Cat, crawls → predicts position 3;
  • position 3 (out) sees Cat, crawls, out → predicts position 4.

With this same single pass, then, several training pairs arise instead of just one — and that is the whole saving.

Reason 2 — the KV-cache during generation. Because the keys and values of each token depend only on it and on the tokens before it, not on later ones, they can be computed once during generation, stored and reused (the KV-cache, section 10.5). Without the causal structure, adding each new token would change the representations of all the others and such caching would not be possible. The mask moreover unifies the behaviour at training and at generation: because each position always depends only on its own past, the model behaves the same whether we present it the whole sentence at once (training) or have it generate token by token (run time).

The solution: mask out future positions. It suffices to intervene in the attention matrix $A$ (the weights). Before the softmax is applied to the rows, we set the score of all forbidden pairs (where column $j$ lies to the right of row $i$, i.e. $j>i$) to $-\infty$. After the softmax, exactly zero comes out of these places, so token $i$ distributes its attention only among positions $1\dots i$. The mask thus zeroes out the entire upper triangle of the weight matrix (Fig. 20).

Bidirectional vs causal mode. The mask is thus a switch between two philosophies. Without the mask, attention is bidirectional — suitable for tasks where we have the whole text available at once and want to understand it (classification, finding an answer in the text). This is the mode of BERT-type models [ref_7]. With the mask, attention is causal — the standard choice when we want a model that generates left to right, learns efficiently, and can use a KV-cache (see 10.2). This is the mode of GPT-type models [ref_8]. We will return to the architecture families in section 11.

10.3The output: unembedding, logits, softmax

From representation to word. At the output of the last block we have the matrix $Z_N$, each row of which is a rich contextual representation of one token. If we want to predict the next token, we take the row corresponding to the last position, since with a causal mask only it contains the contextual representation from the whole original prompt. Let us denote this last row of the matrix $Z_N$ by the letter $z$. From this row we must produce probabilities for the following token, across the entire vocabulary $\mathcal{V}$.

Unembedding = embedding in reverse. Recall that at the input the embedding table $E$ assigned a vector to each token. Here we do exactly the opposite: we ask which token $z$ is closest to. We therefore compute the dot product of $z$ with the vector of every vocabulary token; the result is a vector of logits $o$ of length $|\mathcal{V}|$:

$$o \;=\; z\,W_U, \qquad P(i) \;=\; \mathrm{softmax}(o)_i \;=\; \frac{e^{o_i}}{\sum_{j\in\mathcal{V}} e^{o_j}}.$$

$z$ — the representation of the last position (a row of $Z_N$); $W_U$ — the unembedding matrix of size $d\times|\mathcal{V}|$; $o$ — the vector of logits, $o_i$ being the "raw score" of the $i$-th vocabulary token (the index $i$ runs over the whole vocabulary $\mathcal{V}$, $j$ is the summation index in the denominator of the softmax); $|\mathcal{V}|$ — the vocabulary size; $P(i)$ — the probability that the next token is token $i$.

Weight tying. The logit of token $i$ is the higher, the better $z$ points toward this token's vector. It is therefore natural to take as the unembedding directly the transpose of the input embedding table, $W_U=E^\top$. This sharing of weights is called weight tying: it saves parameters and unifies the "space of meanings" at input and output — a token is encoded and decoded with the same dictionary of vectors.

Why all this works — and why the model does not repeat the last word. At this step it is worth pausing, because it hides the core of all attention. The vector $z$ is not some abstract context hanging in a vacuum: it is the original static embedding of the last token, enriched with context from the previous words. This context gradually transforms — or "bends" — the embedding of the last token toward the embeddings of those tokens that could with high probability follow the given prompt. Each word enters as an isolated vector from the table $E$ — the word "crown" has at first the same vector in a sentence about a king and in a sentence about an exchange rate. Only by passing through the layers do pieces of information from the relevant words get added to it, through the attention layers and residual connections ($z=x+C$, section 6), and its direction shifts.

Here a natural worry arises: when we compute the logit as the dot product of $z$ with the vocabulary embeddings, won't simply the last token itself always win? For the prompt "The British King Charles III ascended the…" that would mean the absurd "…the the". The answer is no — and the reason is instructive: the context "bends" the original token so strongly that essentially only a grammatical skeleton remains of its original identity, and the semantic centre of gravity moves elsewhere.

Example — how "the" becomes "throne"

Prompt "The British King Charles III ascended the…"; the last token is the article the. Let us follow its vector across the layers (each adds its correction):

  • at the input it is just the bare article the — a vector from the table $E$;
  • lower layers link it with the verb ascended;
  • further layers add the context King and British;
  • at the output (row $z$ in $Z_N$) the vector already points in its direction toward that which a British monarch usually ascends; of the original article "the" at most a grammatical trace remains in it.

That the dot product in the end picks throne, and not the, is arranged by three things:

  1. Accumulation through residuals. The residual connections in each layer add corrections, so the original "the" component forms only a small fraction of the resulting $z$ — the representation is dominated by the accumulated context.
  2. A shift of direction. Geometrically, $z$ migrates from the neighbourhood of articles into the region where words like throne, seat, position lie. It points where the context pushed it, not where it started.
  3. Learned suppression of repetition. From the data the model has learned valency: an article like "the" is followed by a noun, not by another "the". It therefore dampens features leading to immediate repetition and strengthens those that point at the expected target.

The dot product of $z$ with the embedding of the word the therefore comes out low (semantically $z$ is already elsewhere), whereas with throne it comes out high — and the softmax turns it into a high probability. The model outputs throne.

This also clarifies two elegances of the whole design. Preservation of position: because the model modifies the vector precisely at the last position, it "knows" that it is generating the word that should follow immediately after it. Preservation of dimension: the vector $z$ has at the end the same dimension $d$ as at the beginning, so the same dictionary $E$ can be applied to it and the logits obtained with a single dot product — which is exactly what weight tying ($W_U=E^\top$) exploits.

A note on architecture — share weights, or not?

Whether to tie the last classification layer to the input table ($W_U=E^\top$), or give it its own independently trained matrix, is a real design decision with palpable consequences.

With weight tying. The model reads and generates words in one and the same geometric space. This brings a large saving of parameters (and thereby of memory) — in smaller models the embedding matrix can easily make up over two thirds of all the weights — and acts as a strong semantic regularization. The price is pressure on the inner layers: they must bend and transform the context vector $z$ so contortedly that at the end it hits exactly the fixed coordinates of the target word in the matrix $E$. (Research even suggests that the shared matrix during training adapts mainly to its output, predictive role, somewhat at the expense of the quality of the input representation.) Weight tying was introduced already by the original Transformer and is used by e.g. GPT-2, BERT and Gemma.

Without weight tying (untied embeddings). The output matrix gains full autonomy and $d\times|\mathcal{V}|$ independent parameters extra — typically on the order of hundreds of millions (e.g. in GPT-3 roughly 617 million; in the very largest models with large $d$ and an extensive vocabulary it approaches a billion). The input and output vectors of the same token do remain geometrically very close, but the output matrix can deform slightly and come "to meet" the inner transformations. This eases for the previous layers that rigid geometric gymnastics and allows the network to separate more finely the semantic understanding of the text at the input from the grammatical and distributional regularities of generation at the output. At the cost of more parameters it is therefore the prevailing choice in today's largest models — e.g. LLaMA 3, Mistral, OLMo 2, DeepSeek-V3 and Qwen3.

10.4Choosing the output token

From the distribution to a concrete word. The softmax gave a probability distribution $P$ over the whole vocabulary; now we must select one concrete token for the model to output. There are two separate questions in this: (1) by what mechanism we draw one value from the distribution at all, and (2) how we possibly adjust that distribution to control the balance between reliability and originality. Let us solve them in turn.

The simplest choice: greedy. We can simply take the token with the highest probability (argmax). It is deterministic and fast, but the output tends to be flat and repetitive — the model slides toward the most probable phrases and repeats itself. Therefore one usually samples instead: the winning token is drawn at random, but in proportion to its probability.

How one draws from a discrete distribution (inverse CDF, the "roulette"). The mechanism is entirely general — the same for any discrete random variable with a known distribution. Imagine a segment of length 1, i.e. the interval $[0,1)$, and divide it into as many pieces as there are tokens, where the length of a piece equals the probability of the corresponding token. We line the pieces up one after another — the boundaries between them are in fact the cumulative sums of the probabilities. Then we draw a single random number $\gamma$ uniformly from $[0,1)$ and look at which piece it fell into; that token we declare selected. Formally, we take the smallest index $i$ for which the cumulative sum $\sum_{j\le i} P(j) \ge \gamma$ (Fig. 21).

$\gamma$ — a random number uniformly distributed on $[0,1)$; $P(i)$ — the probability of the $i$-th token (from section 10.3). A token with greater probability occupies a longer piece, so $\gamma$ falls into it more often — which is why one draws in proportion to the probability, not uniformly.

Greedy is now seen as a limiting case of the same thing: if we cared only about certainty, we would always take the largest piece. Sampling, by contrast, occasionally lands even on a weaker candidate — and from precisely there comes the diversity and "creativity" of the output.

Shaping the distribution before drawing. The second question: before we draw $\gamma$, we can deliberately reshape the distribution $P$ itself, and thereby control how bold the draw will be. Three levers are commonly combined:

  • Temperature $T$. We divide the logits before the softmax by a number $T>0$: $\mathrm{softmax}(o/T)$. A small $T$ ($<1$) sharpens the distribution (the differences between probabilities grow — the model is conservative, surer choices), a large $T$ ($>1$) flattens it (the differences are smoothed out — more randomness and creativity). $T\to 0$ degenerates exactly into greedy.  $T$ — the sampling temperature; the only place in the text where we use the symbol $T$.
  • Top-$k$. We keep only the $k$ most probable tokens, give the rest zero, and normalize the distribution. It cuts off the long tail of nonsensical possibilities.
  • Top-$p$ (nucleus). We keep the smallest set of the most probable tokens whose sum of probabilities exceeds a threshold $p$ (e.g. $0{.}9$), and normalize again. The size of the set adapts — for a confident prediction it is small, for an uncertain one larger.

Shaping (temperature, top-$k$, top-$p$) thus merely changes the shape of the segment from the figure — it enlarges some pieces, shrinks or entirely removes others. The actual selection then always proceeds by the same roulette: $\gamma$ is drawn and the token into whose piece it fell is taken. This is the main lever by which the user tunes the balance between the reliability and the originality of the output.

10.5Autoregression and the KV-cache

Generation as a loop. The model generates one token, appends it to the end of the input, and repeats the whole process for the next position. This "the output becomes the next input" is called autoregression. In this way text arises word by word, until a special end token is drawn or a length limit is reached.

The speed problem. Naively, with each new token we would recompute the attention matrix and the whole output of the attention layer for the entire sequence again — that is work proportional to $n^2$ for a sequence of length $n$, and repeatedly. Yet the keys $k_j$ and values $v_j$ of the already generated tokens do not change; only a new query is added.

The KV-cache. Therefore we store the once-computed keys and values in memory (the KV-cache) and at the next step only compute the key and value of the new token and add them. The cost per step thereby drops from $\mathcal{O}(n^2)$ to $\mathcal{O}(n)$ and generating long texts becomes practically feasible. This is also why a causal (rather than prefix bidirectional) attention layer is so advantageous for generation — see the note in section 11.

Why the past remains unchanged. That the keys and values of old tokens do not change is not obvious — and precisely here it becomes clear why the KV-cache needs the causal mask. For the first layer it is evident: its $K$ and $V$ arise from the static input embeddings, which do not change with the arrival of a new word. But the inputs to the second and higher layers are themselves the output of an attention layer — they depend on the surrounding tokens through the weight matrices $A$ from the lower layers. Without the causal mask, the arrival of a new word would retroactively change the context of past words in the lower layers, and thereby their representations in the higher layers, and the previously stored cache would suddenly cease to be valid. The causal mask, however, mathematically guarantees that information flows only left to right: the past is unchanging in time, so the once-computed $k$ and $v$ of old tokens may be safely stored and recycled in further steps.

One generation step concretely. The whole autoregressive generation then shrinks to little work. For the newly added token we compute — in each layer — only its own triple $q, k, v$ and immediately write the new $k, v$ into the cache alongside the old ones. Then we take the single new query $q$ of the current token and multiply by it the whole key matrix $K$ from the updated cache; the result, after scaling and the softmax, gives one new row of attention weights, by which we multiply the value matrix $V$. The old queries of past tokens we no longer need for anything — they expressed only the questions of past positions toward their then-past and long ago fulfilled their role. In each step, then, only that one newly arrived token passes through the linear projections: its $q$ serves for a single new row of the attention matrix and is then discarded, while $k, v$ remain in memory for the coming rounds.

10.6Training: next-token prediction and self-supervision

Where the "correct answers" come from. Training a transformer as a language model has an ingeniously simple goal: predict the next token. The beauty is that the correct answer need not be annotated by hand — it is simply the actually following token in the text. The targets thus arise from the data themselves by shifting the input one position; this is called self-supervised learning and allows training on enormous corpora without human labels.

The loss function. For each position the model emits a distribution $P(\cdot)$ and we want it to assign the highest possible probability to the actual next token $w^\star$. We measure this by cross-entropy, which we sum over all positions:

$$\mathcal{L} \;=\; -\sum_{i} \log P\big(w^\star_i \mid w_1,\dots,w_i\big).$$

$\mathcal{L}$ — the loss that training minimizes; $w^\star_i$ — the actual token following position $i$; $P(\cdot\mid\cdot)$ — the probability predicted by the model. Thanks to the causal mask, the loss for all positions can be computed in a single pass at once.

The gradients of this loss are sent by backpropagation into all the weights (attention projections, FFN, embeddings…) and the optimizer adjusts them in small steps. The quality and cleanliness of the data are decisive here: the model learns exactly those patterns that are in the corpus — including errors and biases — so enormous effort is devoted to the preparation and cleaning of the training data.

11.Architecture families and why models differ

From the same building blocks — attention layers, feed-forward networks, residuals and normalizations — three large families of models can be built. They differ mainly in how they set up attention (bidirectional vs causal) and how they are trained.

Encoder-only (bidirectional), e.g. BERT [ref_7], ViT [ref_11]. The attention matrix is without a causal mask, each token sees the whole input. It suits understanding: classification, finding an answer in text, features for further tasks. These models are typically pretrained by the task of masked language modelling (MLM): some tokens in the text are randomly hidden and the model is to fill them in from the context on both sides.

Decoder-only (causal), e.g. GPT [ref_8], LLaMA. The attention matrix has a causal mask, generation goes left to right. This is today the dominant family of large language models. They are trained by next-token prediction (section 10.6); a variant is fill-in-the-middle (FIM), where the model learns to fill in an omitted middle while preserving left-to-right generation (useful e.g. in code completion).

Encoder-decoder, e.g. T5 [ref_18], BART. It combines both: a bidirectional encoder processes the input (e.g. a sentence to translate) and a causal decoder generates the output, reading the encoder's representations via cross-attention. Natural for "input sequence → output sequence" tasks (translation, summarization).

Prefix-LM is an intermediate stage: it looks at the introductory "prefix" (the prompt) bidirectionally and at the generated part causally. It thereby approaches the behaviour of an encoder-decoder in a single stack (Fig. 22).

Why specific models differ in performance. The architecture itself is today largely settled — the overwhelming majority of differences between models of the same family do not stem from exotic changes in the equations, but from four practical factors: scale (the number of parameters and compute), the amount and quality of the data, the training recipe (optimizer, length of learning, regularization) and post-training (instruction fine-tuning, alignment with preferences). The same skeleton can thus lead to very differently capable models — the magic does not lie in a different formula, but in how large, on what, and how carefully the model is trained.

12.The transformer as a general sequence processor

The core knows no words. Notice in retrospect that in the whole mechanism of attention, FFN, residuals and normalizations nothing specifically linguistic appears anywhere. Everything works with a sequence of vectors and asks "who relates to whom and how should the representation be adjusted". The transformer is thus a domain-neutral sequence processor: as soon as we can convert the input into a sequence of vectors (tokens), we can apply the same architecture to it. Only the tokenization at the input and the head at the output differ.

Images. An image is cut into square tiles (patches, e.g. 16×16 pixels), each is linearly projected to a vector and a sequence arises — this is the Vision Transformer (ViT) [ref_11]. Literally "an image is worth 16×16 words".

Audio. An audio signal is encoded (e.g. via a learned discrete codebook, vector quantization) into a sequence of acoustic tokens, with which the transformer then works just as with words.

Time series. Here there are several choices and they are worth comparing, because they show how flexible the very idea of a "token" is:

  • Patches of values — a segment of the series (e.g. 32 consecutive measurements) is taken as one token. This is how PatchTST [ref_12] and the foundation model TimesFM [ref_13] work. Patches shorten the sequence and give attention directly the local shape of the curve.
  • Discretization into bins — a continuous value is rounded into one of finitely many baskets, whereby the "vocabulary" becomes finite and the task is converted into classifying the next basket (the Chronos approach [ref_14]).
  • Digits as tokens — a number is written as a string of digits and fed to an ordinary language model (LLMTime [ref_15]); it shows that even a universal text model can extrapolate series.
  • Point by point — older approaches took each time step as a separate token; simple, but it gives long sequences and weaker capture of the local shape.

The output need not be a softmax. For language we drove the output through a softmax into probabilities over the vocabulary. For continuous series this is not necessary. TimesFM, for example, solves direct regression: it takes a patch of length 32 at the input and directly predicts a longer future segment (e.g. 128 values) by minimizing the mean squared error (MSE)  MSE — the average of the squares of the differences between the prediction and reality; $t$ here denotes time, a local quantity of this domain. between the prediction and reality — no softmax, no vocabulary. Longer horizons are then composed autoregressively, patch by patch. The same skeleton, a different head and a different objective (Fig. 23).

13.Adapting a pretrained model

A pretrained transformer is an expensive asset — it has learned rich representations from enormous data. We rarely want to start from scratch; rather we want to adapt it to a specific task (classifying reviews, recognizing a disease from a scan, a corporate assistant). Let us look at how to do it — from the simplest attachment of a head to the economical fine-tuning of weights by the LoRA method.

13.1Classification, the [CLS] token and mean-pooling

The problem: how to make one decision from a sequence of vectors. The transformer returns a sequence of representations (one per token), but classification needs one vector summarizing the whole input. How to obtain it?

One option is to add a special aggregation token [CLS] at the start of the input, which by itself carries no meaning but, thanks to attention, "soaks up" information from the whole sequence during the pass; its output representation is then sent to the classification head. This trick was introduced by BERT [ref_7]. The second option is mean-pooling — simply averaging the output vectors of all tokens, i.e. the rows of the final matrix $Z_N$, and sending this average as input to the classification head. The [CLS] token is thus not indispensable; it is just a convenient "slot" where the model can store the aggregation.

And what about causal (generative) models? Both the [CLS] token and mean-pooling suit mainly bidirectional (encoder) models, where each token sees the whole input. For causal models (GPT, LLaMA) it is different: because of the mask, the context from the whole prompt is carried only by the last token — as we saw in section 10.3, it is the only row of the matrix $Z_N$ that has "seen through" the whole sequence. The natural summarizing vector here is therefore precisely the representation of the last token, which we send to the classification head (it is in fact [CLS] moved to the end). Mean-pooling or an aggregation token at the start would be weaker here — early tokens do not see their right side, so their representations are impoverished.

Can a causal model handle analytical tasks too? Yes, and two routes present themselves. The first is the head over the last token described above — the model is fine-tuned just like an encoder, only we take the last position as the summarizing vector. The second is generative classification: we reformulate the task as predicting the next token, say "Review: …  Sentiment:", and let the model generate the word positive or negative. This is how large language models classify without any new head, often even without further training (zero-shot). The price of one-directionality is that tasks requiring full bidirectional context at every token (e.g. tagging individual words) favour encoders; but for a decision about the whole sequence the last token amply suffices for a causal model — and because decoder LLMs dominate today, classification is done this way quite commonly.

13.2Surgery: the backbone and the head (full-FT, linear-probe)

Adaptation has two parts: the backbone — the pretrained transformer producing representations — and the output head — a small new layer (often just linear) converting the representation into the task's output; its concrete form depends on the task (a classification, regression, language/reading head…). Depending on what we let change during training, we get a spectrum:

  • Linear-probe. We freeze the backbone and train only the new head. Very cheap and fast; it verifies how good the representations the backbone already has are. The risk: if the task requires different features than the backbone offers, we hit a ceiling.
  • Full fine-tuning. We unfreeze all the weights of the backbone and the head. The highest quality ceiling, but the most expensive: for each task a complete copy of all (often billions of) parameters arises, and training is demanding on memory and data; moreover there is a risk of forgetting the original abilities.

Between these extremes lies the question: can't most of the quality of full fine-tuning be obtained at a fraction of its cost? The answer is yes — and it is called LoRA.

13.3LoRA — low-rank fine-tuning

The key observation. When we fine-tune a pretrained model on a new task, the change of weights $\Delta W$ relative to the original weights $W_0$ tends to be "simple" — it lies in a surprisingly low-dimensional subspace. In other words, $\Delta W$ has (approximately) low rank. The LoRA (Low-Rank Adaptation) method [ref_10] profits from this: instead of learning the whole dense matrix of changes, we express it as the product of two narrow matrices.

The construction. Let us pick some weight matrix in the model — typically a projection in attention ($W_Q,W_K,W_V,W_O$), or possibly a matrix in the FFN — of size $d\times d$. We freeze it (leave $W_0$ unchanged) and add a bypass to it: a pair of matrices

$$W \;=\; \underbrace{W_0}_{\text{frozen}} \;+\; \Delta W, \qquad \Delta W \;=\; W_{\text{down}}\,W_{\text{up}},$$

where $W_0$ — the original (frozen) matrix; $\Delta W$ — the learned increment; $W_{\text{down}}$ — a matrix of size $d\times r$ contracting the input into a narrow "bottleneck"; $W_{\text{up}}$ — a matrix of size $r\times d$ expanding back to $d$; $r$ — the rank of the adaptation, a small number (typically 8 or 16), so that $r\ll d$. $W_{\text{down}}$ is of size $d\times r$ and $W_{\text{up}}$ of size $r\times d$. The input vector thus passes through two paths: the frozen original layer and at the same time the narrow bypass through $r$ neurons, and the two contributions are summed.

Why this saves so drastically. Full fine-tuning would adjust $d\times d$ numbers; LoRA learns only $d\times r + r\times d = 2dr$ numbers. For $d=4096$ and $r=8$ that is a drop from $\sim 16{.}8$ million to $\sim 65$ thousand parameters per matrix — on the order of a thousand times fewer. To train and store, then, only these tiny "stickers" suffice, while the mighty backbone $W_0$ stays shared and untouched. For ten different tasks we thus do not need ten copies of the whole model, but one backbone and ten small LoRA adapters (Fig. 24).

Initialization and scale. So that the model at the start of training corresponds exactly to the original (the adapter must not break anything right after the start), $W_{\text{up}}$ is initialized to zero — then $\Delta W=0$ and the model behaves like the pretrained one. The contribution is moreover usually multiplied by a scale $\alpha/r$, which allows the "strength" of the adaptation to be tuned independently.  $\alpha$ — the LoRA scale constant; the ratio $\alpha/r$ controls how large an influence the adapter has.

No overhead at deployment. A great advantage: because $\Delta W=W_{\text{down}}W_{\text{up}}$ is an ordinary matrix of the same shape as $W_0$, after fine-tuning it can be added into $W_0$ once and for all and stored as a single matrix. At inference LoRA then adds no extra computation — unlike some other adapters it is "free" in terms of speed.

Purpose and limits. LoRA is today a standard tool whenever we want to cheaply specialize a large model: the tiny adapters train fast, take up little space and switch easily between tasks. But it has its limits too. The low-rank assumption holds well for fine-tuning close to the original task, but worse if we want to instill in the model entirely new abilities or extensive knowledge — for that a small rank does not suffice and one must either raise $r$, adapt more layers, or resort to full fine-tuning. LoRA thus excellently steers and specializes already existing abilities, but does not replace learning what the model cannot do at all.

13.4A "world model" even for classification

To close, one seemingly paradoxical observation. Why, even for mere classification (e.g. "is this review positive?"), is it usually advantageous to start from a model generatively pretrained on next-token prediction? Because in order to predict well what comes next, the model must build a rich internal model of the world — understand syntax, meaning, relations, facts. These representations are then a far more useful basis for any downstream task than anything learned from scratch on a small classification set. Generative pretraining is not "a luxury for chatbots"; it is a way to cheaply obtain universally usable understanding — and only on top of it to then build a concrete head, be it a classification, regression, or generative one.

14.Conclusion

We have walked through the transformer from the first motivation all the way to deployment and fine-tuning — and it is worth looking back at the story as a whole. We began with a problem: older networks (recurrent, convolutional) either processed the sequence step by step and struggled to hold long dependencies, or saw only a local neighbourhood. The transformer answered this with a single but powerful idea — attention — which lets each token reach directly to any other and read from it exactly what it needs.

Around this core we revealed, step by step, why each further part must exist. The projections $W_Q,W_K$ separated relevance from mere similarity; $W_V$ separated what is read from what the weighting is based on; multi-head attention allowed several relations to be tracked at once; positional encoding brought order back into play; the residual connection turned the layers into a sequence of fine corrections of a shared estimate; the FFN supplied nonlinear computation within a token and a store of knowledge; normalization kept the whole deep machine trainable. Their composition produced a block whose repetition turns surface features into abstract meanings.

Finally we saw that the same skeleton is domain-neutral: it works with a sequence of vectors, whether they come from words, image tiles, audio or a time series. Only the tokenization at the input and the head at the output change. And because pretraining is expensive, we learned to adapt the model — from attaching a head, through full fine-tuning, to the economical LoRA, which cheaply specializes a large model without touching its backbone.

Perhaps the most important insight is that behind the apparent complexity of the transformer there is no jumble of ad-hoc tricks, but a few recurring principles — relevance instead of similarity, base plus correction, communication between tokens vs computation within a token, a domain-neutral sequence of vectors. Whoever understands these principles stops seeing a jumble of equations and begins to see an architecture that makes sense. That was the goal of this text; the concrete extensions, optimizations and variants are just further chapters of the same story.
